# Body copy — rhythm + cadence

The headline earns the read. Body copy earns the next sentence. Then the next. Body copy is the slippery slide between the hook and the ask.

## The 3 rhythm laws

### Law 1 — Variance > 4
Sentence-word-count standard deviation must exceed 4. AI writes flat (every sentence 14-16 words). Humans write jagged (3, 18, 8, 14, 2, 21, 6).

If your draft reads flat, the fix isn't word swap — it's sentence-length surgery. Break a 20-word sentence into a 4 and a 16. Merge two 8-word sentences into a 16.

### Law 2 — One idea per sentence
Long sentences carry argument. Short sentences carry punch. Compound sentences with three sub-ideas read as drafts that didn't get edited.

If a sentence has more than one main verb, split it.

### Law 3 — The first word does work
The first word of every paragraph and every sentence is load-bearing. Audit them.
- Verbs and named subjects > articles and adjectives.
- "We" and "Our" as openers = lazy. Replace with the reader, the product, or the action.
- "It" and "There" as openers = even lazier. Rewrite.

## The 6 named cadences

### Cadence 1 — Flat hammer
All short sentences. 3-7 words each. Use sparingly — for a paragraph, not a page. Builds momentum, exhausts the reader if overused.

> We tried that. It didn't work. We tried again. Same result. Then we changed the variable. Worked the third time.

### Cadence 2 — Conversational
Mixed sentence lengths, contractions on, occasional fragments. The default cadence for most copy.

> You've seen this pitch before. Probably from us, even — three years ago, different problem, same shape. The reason it didn't land then was specificity. We had the angle but not the proof. We have the proof now.

### Cadence 3 — Rhythmic punch
Short, short, long. Builds to a payoff. Common in DTC sales letters and manifestos.

> We do one thing.
> We do it well.
> Sixteen years, four hundred clients, every single one referred us to the next one without being asked.

### Cadence 4 — Whisper
Long, restrained sentences with deliberate pauses. For luxury, heritage, premium B2B. Abbott territory.

> The first hour of every project is spent reading what came before. Not your brief. Not the deliverables. The internal Slack threads where the actual disagreement lives.

### Cadence 5 — Direct pitch
Headline, claim, proof, ask. No transitions. Each beat lands hard.

> Three weeks. Fixed price. You leave with a deck no investor's seen before.
> $47M raised in 2025 across 14 decks.
> Reply with your current draft.

### Cadence 6 — Slippery slide (Sugarman)
Each sentence sets up the next. The reader can't stop because every line creates a small open loop.

> We tried that.
> For three years.
> Four people, eight clients, four countries.
> It didn't work.
> Here's why.

## Paragraph architecture

- **Default paragraph length: 2-4 sentences.** Single-sentence paragraphs are fine for emphasis, never as a default.
- **First sentence states or hooks. Last sentence pulls forward.** The middle is the proof or example.
- **Never lead with a transition word** ("However," "Therefore," "Additionally"). The transition belongs in the previous paragraph's last sentence, or it doesn't belong at all.
- **One paragraph per beat.** Don't pack two arguments into one paragraph; the reader skims and loses both.

## Transitions that earn their keep

Most "transitions" are filler. The good ones are short and specific:
- "But" — earns a turn.
- "Then" — earns a sequence.
- "Here's why" — earns the proof beat.
- "The point" / "The catch" / "The trick" — earns a reframe.

Avoid: "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "On the other hand," "In conclusion."

## The 2 worked examples

### Example 1 — Tightening flat copy

BEFORE (flat — variance 1.2):
> Our team brings extensive experience to every project. We work closely with our clients to understand their unique needs. Our approach is collaborative and strategic at every step. We deliver results that exceed expectations consistently.

AFTER (rhythmic — variance 6.8):
> We've done this 47 times. The pattern's the same. You hand us a brief, we read everything else first — internal Slack, old decks, dead drafts. Then we write something nobody saw coming. You sign off, we ship, you raise.

### Example 2 — Building a slippery slide

BEFORE (flat sequence):
> The deck has problems. We can fix it. We have a proven process. Many clients have used it.

AFTER (slide):
> The deck has three problems.
> We can name them in 20 minutes.
> We can fix them in three weeks.
> Last year we did it 14 times. Here's what changed in those decks.
