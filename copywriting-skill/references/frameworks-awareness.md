# Frameworks — Awareness cluster

The deep-end frameworks. Three patterns for long-form copy where the reader's awareness state and flow discipline are the load-bearing variables.

---

## Schwartz 5-stage — Awareness-state matching

The deepest move in copywriting. Built by Eugene Schwartz in *Breakthrough Advertising*. The reader's awareness state determines the lead. Wrong stage = the copy bounces no matter how well-written.

**The 5 stages:**
1. **Unaware.** Reader doesn't know they have a problem. Lead with story or curiosity. Don't sell.
2. **Problem-Aware.** Reader knows the pain, not the solution. Lead with the pain in their words. Promise a different angle.
3. **Solution-Aware.** Reader knows solutions exist; not your solution. Lead with mechanism — why your approach is different.
4. **Product-Aware.** Reader knows your product; hasn't bought. Lead with comparison or new angle on familiar offer.
5. **Most-Aware.** Reader knows you, your product, and is ready. Lead with the offer. No setup. Just terms.

**How to pick a stage:**
- Source of the traffic tells you the stage. Cold paid ads → Stage 1-2. Retargeting → Stage 3-4. Email list → Stage 4-5.
- Read the source-list intent. Search queries with "best X" → Stage 3. Search queries naming your brand → Stage 4-5.
- When in doubt, write to Stage 3 (Solution-Aware). It's the most common state for landing pages.

**Mini example (Stage 3 lead):**
> You've seen the other pitch-deck services. They charge $30k for a slide redesign and call it strategic.
> We rewrite the narrative. The deck reads like a Harvard case, not a brochure.
> Three weeks. Fixed price. You leave with a deck that closes.

**When to pick:** Long-form sales pages, paid-acquisition landings, anywhere the source list's awareness state is identifiable. The most powerful framework when used right.

## Slippery Slide — Sentence-to-sentence flow

Sugarman's discipline. Every sentence's only job is to get the reader to the next. Short → long → short → long. The first sentence is short and intriguing. The second earns more weight. The third gets shorter again.

**Structure:**
- **Lead:** 3-7 word sentence. Often a fragment. Sets curiosity or stops the scroll.
- **Earn:** The next sentence (or two) earns the lead's promise. Specific, concrete.
- **Hook forward:** The end of every paragraph pulls the reader's eye to the start of the next paragraph.
- **No throat-clearing.** Cut any sentence whose only job is transition.

**Beat structure:** Not a fixed number of beats — runs as long as the slide holds. Stops when the reader either converts or bounces.

**When to pick:** Long-form narrative copy. Sales emails over 200 words. Sales letters. Newsletter intros. Anywhere the read needs to flow without friction.

**Mini example:**
> We tried that.
> For three years, with a team of 14, across eight clients in four countries.
> It didn't work.
> Here's what we learned.
> [next paragraph picks up "what we learned"]

The slide is the chain-link between sentences. Every line earns the next.

## SCQA — Situation / Complication / Question / Answer

Executive-narrative framework. Built by Barbara Minto for consultative pitching. Walks a senior decision-maker from "I know this" through "but..." to "here's what you do." Used in McKinsey-style memos for a reason.

**Structure:**
- **Situation.** What is true today. Established fact the reader already accepts.
- **Complication.** What changed or what now contradicts the situation. The "but" that creates the question.
- **Question.** The implicit question the complication raises. Sometimes stated, often inferred.
- **Answer.** Your recommendation. Frames everything downstream of it.

**Beat count:** 4. The setup (Situation + Complication) earns the right to deliver the Answer.

**When to pick:** Executive summary, board memo, consultative pitch deck opening, proposal-doc lead section. Anywhere the reader is senior, time-constrained, and skeptical of being sold to.

**Mini example:**
> Situation: Your pitch deck has been in front of 14 investors. None have committed.
> Complication: The fundamentals are strong. The numbers are real. But the deck reads like every other deck in their pipeline.
> Question: How do you make the deck land in the first 90 seconds?
> Answer: Rewrite the narrative, not the design. Three weeks. We work with two clients at a time.

The Question is often unspoken — but it has to be the question the reader is already asking themselves when they hit the Complication.

## Cluster rules

1. **Schwartz first.** Before voice, before framework, identify the awareness state. If you don't know it, you're guessing.
2. **The slide is a discipline, not a structure.** Slippery Slide doesn't tell you what to write — it tells you how every line connects to the next.
3. **SCQA earns its right to recommend.** The Answer is only as strong as the Situation/Complication earned it.

## When this cluster is wrong

- **Wrong for short copy** — these are long-form patterns. For 50-word ads, use PAS or BAB.
- **Wrong for hype/launch** — Schwartz's slow build dies on a launch beat. Use Wieden's voice over AIDA's structure.
- **Wrong for product detail** — these route narrative, not feature breakdowns. Use FAB.
