# Frameworks — Sales cluster

Section-level and product-level frameworks. Three patterns for moving the reader through a single beat — a feature, a section, or a sales letter — without losing momentum.

---

## 4Ps — Promise / Picture / Proof / Push

Section-level workhorse. Use inside a larger AIDA or Schwartz frame, or as the spine of a single email or landing-page section.

**Structure:**
- **Promise.** The transformation. One sentence. Specific.
- **Picture.** Show the reader the future-state in concrete detail. Sensory if possible.
- **Proof.** Numbers, named cases, dated outcomes. Not testimonials in the abstract.
- **Push.** The ask. Singular, low-friction, time-bound where possible.

**Beat count:** 4. Roughly 1-2-2-1.

**When to pick:** Section inside a longer page. Single-section sales emails. Proposal-doc sub-section.

**Mini example:**
> Promise: We rewrite the pitch deck. Three weeks. You close the round.
> Picture: Investors lean in by slide four. The questions get sharper. The follow-up email arrives Monday morning.
> Proof: $47M raised across 14 decks last year. Three of them closed at the meeting.
> Push: 12 seats this quarter. Reply by Friday.

---

## FAB — Feature / Advantage / Benefit

Product-detail framework. Use for feature-level copy on a product page, spec sheet, or comparison section. Translates technical truth into reader value in three beats.

**Structure:**
- **Feature.** What the product literally has or does. Technical fact.
- **Advantage.** What that feature lets the product do better than alternatives.
- **Benefit.** What that does for the reader's life. The "so what" payoff.

**Beat count:** 3. Roughly even, sometimes 1-1-2 (benefit carries).

**When to pick:** Product pages with multiple features, comparison tables, technical-brief audiences who need translation from spec to value.

**Mini example:**
> Feature: 16 ingredients in the formula.
> Advantage: Twelve are clinically tested. Most competitors use 4-6.
> Benefit: You stop guessing whether the product works. The data is on the label.

## AIDCA — Attention / Interest / Desire / Conviction / Action

AIDA's longer sibling. Adds a conviction beat between Desire and Action — the proof that earns the click. Use for cold lists, high-skepticism audiences, or any sales letter where the reader's main blocker is "do I trust this?"

**Structure:**
- **A — Attention.** Hook.
- **I — Interest.** Earn the read.
- **D — Desire.** Show transformation.
- **C — Conviction.** Proof block. Numbers, named cases, dated outcomes, testimonials with specifics.
- **A — Action.** Singular ask.

**Beat count:** 5. Roughly 1-2-2-3-1 (conviction is the biggest block).

**When to pick:** Cold-list sales letter, paid-acquisition landing page, anything where skepticism is the dominant objection. Long enough that AIDA's 4 beats feel hand-wavy.

**Mini example skeleton:**
> A: Most pitch decks lose investors at slide 3.
> I: You've spent six weeks on yours. The team reviewed it twice. It still feels generic.
> D: Imagine the deck that lands. Investors lean in. Follow-up Monday morning. Term sheet within 30 days.
> C: 14 decks last year. $47M raised. Three closed at the meeting. Named clients: A, B, C. (Add specifics.)
> A: Reply with your current deck. We'll come back in 48 hours with a teardown.

---

## Cluster rules

1. **4Ps lives inside other frameworks.** Use it as a section-level structure inside AIDA or Schwartz 5-stage.
2. **FAB requires technical substance.** Never use FAB on copy where the "Feature" is a fluff word like "innovative" or "powerful."
3. **AIDCA earns its length.** If you can't fill 5 beats with substance, drop to AIDA. AIDCA without real proof is just bloated AIDA.

## When this cluster is wrong

- **Wrong for short attention copy** — these are section-level or long-form. For 8-word hooks, use a headline formula.
- **Wrong for awareness-state long-form** — Schwartz 5-stage handles cold audiences with more precision. Pull `frameworks-awareness.md`.
- **Wrong for executive/strategic narrative** — use SCQA. Pull `frameworks-awareness.md`.
