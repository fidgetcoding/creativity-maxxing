# Voices — Challenger cluster

Zag when the category zigs. Refuse the default move and surprise the reader into paying attention. For brands that need to interrupt a stale category, not extend it.

## John Hegarty — Zag

**Signature:** Find what every other brand in the category is doing. Do the opposite. The interruption IS the campaign.

**Techniques:**
- Audit the category before writing. What's the cliché lead? What's the cliché visual? What's the cliché voice?
- Zag from that cliché on the surface that matters most (headline, visual, tone, channel).
- Don't over-zag. One contrast is the whole campaign. Two contrasts confuse.
- "When the world zigs, zag" — but only zag when zigging is exhausted. If the category's default still works, don't waste the move.

**Exemplars:**
> "When the world zigs, zag." (Levi's, Hegarty himself.)
> Audi "Vorsprung durch Technik" — kept a German phrase untranslated in UK ads. The whole category translated; Audi didn't. The phrase became the brand.
> Häagen-Dazs adult ice cream — the category was selling to kids; Häagen-Dazs sold to couples. One zag, owned the premium tier.

**Pick Hegarty when:** Launch into a saturated category. Repositioning a brand against a stale leader. Any brief where the safe move would extend the category cliché.

## Tom McElligott — Smart-simple-surprising

**Signature:** Smart. Simple. Surprising. Refuse the default at the sentence level — not just the campaign level. Every line is one beat away from where the reader expected it to go.

**Techniques:**
- Set up the expected line. Deliver an unexpected one.
- Use parallel structure to lull the reader, then break the parallel on the final beat.
- One specific concrete image per ad. Never a stock idea.
- Mid-market accessibility — Hegarty zags loud, McElligott zags quiet. Same instinct, lower volume.

**Exemplars:**
> "The bigger they are, the harder we work." (an Avis-adjacent challenger line, McElligott in spirit.)
> "We're not your father's Oldsmobile." (challenger-tier reposition — admits the category liability, claims the reframe.)
> "Hot dogs. Armour Hot Dogs. What kind of kids eat Armour Hot Dogs? Fat kids, skinny kids, kids who climb on rocks..." (jingle version of the parallel-then-break move — set up, set up, set up, surprise.)

**Pick McElligott when:** Mid-market brand that needs a punch but can't afford full Hegarty volume. Series of ads where each line has to land its own beat. Editorial or campaign copy with sentence-by-sentence wit.

## When this cluster is wrong

- **Wrong for trust-building** — challenger voice reads as cocky on a founder letter. Use Bernbach.
- **Wrong for compliance/legal/heritage** — the zag against your own brand reads as instability. Use Abbott.
- **Wrong for DTC sales letters** — challenger surprise breaks the slippery slide. Use Sugarman.

## Cluster-wide rules

1. **Audit before zagging.** Without naming the cliché you're refusing, the zag has no contrast and reads as random.
2. **One zag per campaign.** Hegarty's whole career: every campaign has ONE contrast. The discipline is the craft.
3. **Specificity beats irony.** A specific surprising detail (Audi keeping German) lands. Generic irony ("we're different!") falls flat.
4. **The zag earns the safe stuff.** Once the reader sees you refused the cliché, every other line gets read with more attention.
5. **Don't zag when zigging works.** If the category lead is genuinely strong, joining the consensus may be the right move. Save the challenger for tired categories.
