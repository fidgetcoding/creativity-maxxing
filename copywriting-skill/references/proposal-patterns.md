# Proposal patterns — the 7-section pitch doc

A proposal lives in a different register than a sales letter. The reader is senior, time-constrained, and skeptical of being sold to. The doc has to feel inevitable, not pitched.

## The 7-section structure

Most strong B2B proposal docs collapse to this 7-beat sequence. Skip a section only with reason.

### Section 1 — The reader's situation (SCQA Situation)
Open with a sentence the reader already accepts as true. Establishes shared ground before any claim.

> You've raised once. The first round closed at $4M. The second is targeting $14M, and the deck that worked for the first round won't work for this one.

**Length:** 30-80 words. **Voice:** flat, observational.

### Section 2 — The complication
What changed, or what now contradicts the situation. The "but" that creates the question. This is where you earn the right to recommend anything.

> But the bar is different now. You're not pitching the idea — you're pitching execution. Investors at this stage want pattern recognition, not promise. The deck has to read like a case study, not a brochure.

**Length:** 50-120 words. **Voice:** measured, specific.

### Section 3 — The recommendation
Your answer. One paragraph. Singular. Concrete.

> We rewrite the narrative arc. Three weeks. Fixed price. You leave with a deck that closes the round.

**Length:** 30-80 words. **Voice:** confident, no hedge.

### Section 4 — The mechanism (how this works)
Show the work. Specific process beats vague claims about "approach" or "methodology." Three to five named steps with concrete deliverables.

> Step 1: We read everything. Internal Slack, old decks, dead drafts, board notes. Three days.
> Step 2: We write the new arc. Cold draft on day six. Revisions on day ten.
> Step 3: We pressure-test with a friendly investor in your network. Day twelve.
> Step 4: Final draft + presenter notes. Day fifteen.

**Length:** 100-200 words. **Voice:** declarative, sequential.

### Section 5 — The proof
Named clients (with permission), specific numbers, dated outcomes. Not testimonials in the abstract — proof that the mechanism above produces the results claimed.

> Last year: 14 decks. $47M raised. Three closed at the meeting. Named: A, B, C (refs available).
> Median rewrite delivers 38% reduction in slide count and a 60% reduction in investor follow-up questions.

**Length:** 80-150 words. **Voice:** specific, lean.

### Section 6 — The terms
Price, timeline, deliverables, what's in, what's out. No hedging. No "starting at." Singular.

> Three weeks, $24,000, fixed. Includes: narrative rewrite, presenter notes, one revision round. Excludes: design, animation, slide template work.

**Length:** 40-100 words. **Voice:** flat, transactional.

### Section 7 — The ask + the close
Singular next step. Optional disqualifier (Bernbach close). Optional guarantee (Sackheim close).

> Reply with your current deck. We'll come back in 48 hours with the teardown.
> If we're not the fit, we'll say so.

**Length:** 20-60 words. **Voice:** direct, low-friction.

## Total length target

The whole doc should be 500-900 words. Anything over 1,200 is a sign you didn't trim. Anything under 400 reads as thin.

## Worked skeleton (200-word minimum proposal)

> You've raised once. The first round closed at $4M; the second is targeting $14M. The deck that worked for the first round won't work for this one.
>
> But the bar is different now. Investors at this stage want pattern recognition, not promise — the deck has to read like a case study, not a brochure.
>
> We rewrite the narrative. Three weeks. Fixed price. You leave with a deck that closes.
>
> The work runs four steps. We read everything (three days). We write the cold draft (day six). We pressure-test with a friendly investor (day twelve). We deliver final + presenter notes (day fifteen).
>
> Last year: 14 decks. $47M raised. Three closed at the meeting. Named clients available on request.
>
> Three weeks, $24,000, fixed. Includes the rewrite, presenter notes, one revision round. Excludes design.
>
> Reply with your current deck. We'll come back in 48 hours with a teardown. If we're not the fit, we'll say so.

## Anti-patterns

- **"About Us" lead.** The reader doesn't care about your team yet. Earn that with the recommendation first.
- **Stacked services menu.** Three packages dilutes the ask. Pick the right offer for this client and lead with it.
- **Vague mechanism.** "Strategic," "collaborative," "iterative" — all dead. Replace with named steps.
- **Hedged price.** "Starting at," "depending on scope," "let's discuss." Reads as unconfident. Pick a number.
- **Generic proof.** "Trusted by leading brands" — meaningless. Name them or skip the line.
- **No close.** Doc trails off into "looking forward to discussing." Always end with a singular next step.

## When this pattern is wrong

- **Wrong for cold-list outreach.** Use a sales letter (AIDCA or Schwartz 5-stage), not a proposal.
- **Wrong for retainer renewals.** Drop sections 1-2 (situation + complication are already shared) and lead with the recommendation.
- **Wrong for design-led pitches.** When the visual concept IS the pitch, the doc is a frame for the visual; words shrink.
