# Psychological triggers — the persuasion stack

The decision happens in the limbic system. The justification happens in the cortex. Strong copy gives the reader both — emotion to decide, logic to defend the decision.

## Cialdini's 7 principles

The Cialdini set is the canon. Use as a checklist before shipping any persuasive copy.

| # | Principle | How it shows up in copy |
|---|-----------|-------------------------|
| 1 | **Reciprocity** | Give before you ask. Free audit, free brief, free chapter. The ask comes after value lands. |
| 2 | **Commitment & Consistency** | Get a small yes before the big one. "Reply if interested" → "Send your deck" → "Book the call." |
| 3 | **Social proof** | Specific named clients. Specific numbers. Specific dated outcomes. Generic testimonials don't count. |
| 4 | **Liking** | Sound like a human. Specificity, occasional self-deprecation, opinions held with confidence. The Bernbach voice is liking weaponized. |
| 5 | **Authority** | Demonstrated expertise, not claimed expertise. Show the work. Cite specific cases. Avoid credential-listing as a default. |
| 6 | **Scarcity** | Real, not fake. Named cohort sizes, real deadlines, capacity caps. "12 seats. Friday." not "limited time only!" |
| 7 | **Unity** | Shared identity with the reader. "Founders who've raised once" or "operators who've shipped at scale." Identity beats demographics. |

**Use 3+ per long-form piece.** Less than 3 reads thin. More than 5 reads stacked.

## Sugarman's top 10 (from *Triggers*)

Sugarman cataloged 30+ psychological triggers in direct response. The 10 most load-bearing:

1. **Curiosity gap.** Open a loop, close it later. The unread next sentence is the conversion.
2. **Specificity.** A specific number ($47M, 12 seats, 30 days) beats a generic claim every time. Specificity = credibility.
3. **Honesty / authenticity.** Admit the flaw. Reader trusts the rest.
4. **Storytelling.** A narrative beats a feature list. The reader places themselves inside it.
5. **Sense of urgency.** Real time pressure earns the click. Fake urgency burns the brand.
6. **Greed / opportunity.** Make the upside specific and visible.
7. **Authority / credibility.** Named cases, named clients, named numbers.
8. **Satisfaction conviction.** Risk reversal. Guarantee. "Try it. If it doesn't work, send it back."
9. **Linking.** Connect the new offer to something the reader already knows or owns.
10. **Consistency.** Each step reinforces the prior commitment. The slippery slide is consistency in motion.

## Schwartz's 5 awareness states (deeper treatment)

Recap of `frameworks-awareness.md`. Stage match is the highest-leverage move in persuasion copy.

1. **Unaware** — story / curiosity. Don't sell.
2. **Problem-Aware** — name the pain. Promise an angle.
3. **Solution-Aware** — show mechanism. Differentiate.
4. **Product-Aware** — comparison or new angle on familiar offer.
5. **Most-Aware** — just the offer.

Wrong-stage copy bounces. Always identify the stage before writing the lead.

## Schwartz's 5 sophistication stages

A second Schwartz axis. Not awareness of YOUR product — awareness of CLAIMS in your category.

1. **Stage 1.** Reader has never seen this claim before. Make it big and direct.
2. **Stage 2.** Reader has heard the claim. Make it bigger.
3. **Stage 3.** Reader is tired of the claim. Add a unique mechanism.
4. **Stage 4.** Reader is tired of the mechanism. Elaborate the mechanism.
5. **Stage 5.** Reader is tired of everything. Identify with them. Become the brand that knows the category is exhausted.

Most B2B advisory and DTC categories are Stage 4-5 today. Stage-3 claim language reads as desperate.

## Life Force 8 (Drew Eric Whitman)

The eight desires every human has, hardwired:

1. Survival, enjoyment of life, life extension.
2. Enjoyment of food and beverages.
3. Freedom from fear, pain, danger.
4. Sexual companionship.
5. Comfortable living conditions.
6. To be superior, winning, keeping up with the Joneses.
7. Care and protection of loved ones.
8. Social approval.

Most B2B copy hits #6 and #8. Most DTC copy hits #1, #5, #6, #7. Identify which of the 8 your offer activates, and write to it directly.

If your copy doesn't connect to one of the 8, the offer is too abstract to convert.

## Trigger stacking — how to combine

A strong long-form piece stacks 3-5 of the above into a single read. A typical stack:

1. **Open with curiosity** (Sugarman trigger 1) + **specificity** (Sugarman trigger 2).
2. **Build with proof** (Cialdini authority + social proof).
3. **Reframe with mechanism** (Schwartz Stage 3+ sophistication move).
4. **Close with risk reversal** (Sugarman trigger 8).
5. **Disqualify the wrong reader** (Bernbach honesty + Cialdini scarcity).

Don't stack more than 5 in a single piece. The reader feels the pile-on, the brand starts to read as needy.

## The 3 ethical guardrails

1. **No fake scarcity.** Real cohort caps and real deadlines only.
2. **No false claims.** If the proof isn't real, the brand is fragile. Specificity has to be true.
3. **No fear-based manipulation that misrepresents stakes.** "If you don't buy this, your career ends" is unethical. "If you don't fix the deck, you raise less" is fact.

The reader's bullshit detector is sharper than copywriters typically estimate. Manipulation gets caught and remembered.
