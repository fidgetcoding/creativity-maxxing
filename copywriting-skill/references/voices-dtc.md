# Voices — DTC cluster

Direct response. Sales letters. Anything where the reader has to convert in one read. The cluster runs on three engines: awareness-state matching (Schwartz), slippery slide (Sugarman), and guarantee-up-front psychology (Sackheim).

## Joe Sugarman — Slippery slide

**Signature:** Every sentence's only job is to get the reader to the next sentence. The first sentence is short — usually 3-7 words — and it earns the second. Momentum is everything.

**Techniques:**
- Open with a short, intriguing sentence. Often a fragment.
- Second sentence pulls weight: explains, deepens, or escalates.
- Use white space as visual breath. Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph.
- Stack curiosity gaps: drop a fact, promise to explain, then deliver the explanation only after another curiosity hook.

**Exemplars:**
> "I tried that. For three years. With four people, eight clients, four countries. It didn't work. Here's why."
> (Sugarman's BluBlocker copy opens: "Now you can be a hero." Then earns it across 1,200 words.)

**Pick Sugarman when:** Long-form sales letter, landing page narrative, email body that has to walk the reader through a sequence of beats. Anywhere abandonment risk is high and you need flow.

## Maxwell Sackheim — Guarantee up front

**Signature:** Lead with the reversal-of-risk. "Try this. If you don't love it, send it back. No questions, no shipping, no hard feelings." The guarantee opens the letter, not closes it.

**Techniques:**
- The guarantee is the lead. Often the first or second sentence.
- Specificity in the guarantee: "30 days." "Send it back media-mail, we cover the return." "Keep the bonuses either way."
- Restate the guarantee at the close, exactly the same, so the reader can re-read both bookends.
- Stack the offer before the price. The price is the smallest thing on the page.

**Exemplars:**
> "Send no money. Just mail the coupon. If you don't agree this is the best book you've read all year, return it within 30 days and owe nothing."
> "Do you make these mistakes in English?" (Sherwin Cody / Sackheim — opens with a question that names the reader's specific pain, then promises the fix with a guarantee.)

**Pick Sackheim when:** Cold list, high-skepticism audience, anything where the buyer's main objection is "what if this doesn't work?" — strip that objection on line one.

## Eugene Schwartz — Awareness-state matching

**Signature:** Meet the reader where they are. Five awareness states (Unaware → Problem-Aware → Solution-Aware → Product-Aware → Most-Aware). The hook changes per state. Wrong state = the copy bounces.

**Techniques:**
- **Stage 1 (Unaware):** Lead with a story. The reader doesn't know they have a problem yet. Don't sell — entertain.
- **Stage 2 (Problem-Aware):** Name the pain. "You keep doing X and it keeps not working." Promise a different angle.
- **Stage 3 (Solution-Aware):** Show your solution's mechanism. "Here's why our approach is different."
- **Stage 4 (Product-Aware):** Direct comparison. "You know us. Here's the offer."
- **Stage 5 (Most-Aware):** Just the offer. No setup. "30% off. 48 hours. Code XYZ."

**Exemplars:**
> Schwartz's *Breakthrough Advertising* — every example in the book is a re-staged headline showing the same product pitched to readers at different awareness states.
> The classic: pitching a weight-loss product to someone who knows they're overweight is Stage 2; pitching it to someone who's already tried 4 programs is Stage 4.

**Pick Schwartz when:** Long-form sales page, paid-acquisition landing page, anywhere you can identify the source-list awareness state. **This is the deepest move in this cluster** — if you skip the state match, every other technique compounds wrong.

## When this cluster is wrong

- **Wrong for luxury/restraint** — DTC's volume and stack reads as cheap on heritage brands. Use Abbott.
- **Wrong for B2B advisory** — Sackheim's guarantee leads as gimmicky on enterprise sales. Use Bencivenga.
- **Wrong for brand-launch energy** — Sugarman's flow is too explanatory for a launch hype. Use Wieden.

## Cluster-wide rules

1. **Awareness state first.** Before voice, before framework. If you don't know which of Schwartz's 5 the reader is in, you're writing in the dark.
2. **The lead is the conversion.** 80% of DTC copy reads (or skips) on the lead alone. Spend 50% of the writing time there.
3. **Risk reversal earns the click.** Without a guarantee or a clear out, skepticism wins.
4. **Specificity stacks.** Numbers, names, dollars, dates. Generic claims in DTC are dead claims.
5. **One offer per page.** Two offers in a sales letter halve the conversion of both.
