# Voices — Punch cluster

Direct, verb-driven, fast. For B2B advisory, briefs that need momentum, copy that has to fight for attention. The cluster's signature: every line moves the reader one step forward. No throat-clearing.

## Dave Trott — Punch-first

**Signature:** "Simple. Interesting. Relevant." Verb or named subject as the first word. The pitch is the first sentence.

**Techniques:**
- Opening with a verb or a named subject. Never with "We" or "Our" or "In today's."
- Three-beat structure: setup / turn / payoff. Three sentences max in a punch.
- Subject-verb-object. No subordinate clauses in the lead.
- Names beat descriptions. "Nike" beats "the athletic apparel category leader."

**Exemplars:**
> "Predictability is the killer of advertising."
> "If you can't be funny, be relevant. If you can't be relevant, be useful. If you can't be useful, get out of advertising."
> "Direct rather than clever. Simple rather than smart. Relevant rather than artistic."

**Pick Trott when:** B2B pitch, advisory deliverable, anything that has 8 seconds to land. Defaults to here when in doubt.

## Gary Bencivenga — Proof-first

**Signature:** Address the skeptic before making the claim. Three proofs before one assertion. The reader's objection appears on the page before you offer your answer.

**Techniques:**
- Lead with the skepticism: "You've heard this before. Three other firms told you the same thing last quarter. Here's why we're different."
- Stack proofs in groups of three (named clients, specific numbers, dated cases). Then claim.
- Long-form is fine — proof requires room. But every paragraph passes the "would I bet money this is true" test.
- Specific over generic. "$4.7M in client revenue, 2025" beats "drove significant revenue."

**Exemplars:**
> "If you want to convince a skeptic, show three proofs before you make one claim. They're not waiting to be told. They're waiting to be shown."
> "The most powerful word in advertising is not 'free.' It's 'proof.'"

**Pick Bencivenga when:** B2B sales letter, high-stakes pitch, audience knows your category and is tired of pitches. Long-form direct response.

## Dan Wieden — Verb-driven energy

**Signature:** Chip off the cosmic block. Verb-first lines that sound like they were said aloud and worth writing down. For the ear, not the eye.

**Techniques:**
- Imperative verbs as line openers: "Just do it." "Stop. Listen. Run."
- Short lines, often single words on a line break. Visual rhythm doubles as audio rhythm.
- Repetition with variation, not parallel structure: "Find your line. Run your line. Own your line."
- Action verbs over state verbs. "Move" not "be moving." "Run" not "be running."

**Exemplars:**
> "Just do it."
> "Find your greatness."
> "It's only crazy until you do it."

**Pick Wieden when:** Launch copy, anthem, brand-film voiceover, manifesto, anything that gets read aloud. Athletic, defiant, or aspirational brands.

## When this cluster is wrong

- **Wrong for luxury restraint** — Wieden's energy fights white-space brands. Use Abbott.
- **Wrong for founder honesty** — Trott's punch-first feels too sold for a confession letter. Use Bernbach.
- **Wrong for awareness-state DTC** — Bencivenga's proof works but the slippery-slide structure isn't built in here. Use Schwartz or Sugarman.

## Cluster-wide rules

1. Verbs do the lifting. Adjectives are decoration.
2. The first word of every paragraph is load-bearing. Audit it.
3. Three is the structural beat — sentences, proofs, repetitions. Resist four.
4. Read aloud or don't ship. Punch is an audio property.
