# Headlines — formulas + craft

The headline does 80% of the work. If it doesn't earn the read, nothing downstream matters. Target 3-10 words. Read aloud or don't ship.

## The 12 master patterns

Most strong headlines collapse to one of these 12 structural moves.

### 1. The how-to
"How to [achieve outcome] without [common cost]"
> How to write a deck that closes — without rewriting your whole pitch.

### 2. The specific number
"[Number] [things] that [outcome]"
> Seven moves that make a cold pitch land.

### 3. The pointed question
A question the target reader is already asking themselves.
> What's wrong with your hero section?
> Why is your conversion rate flat?

### 4. The contrarian claim
A reversal of conventional wisdom.
> Specificity beats persuasion. Always.
> Your deck doesn't need a redesign. It needs a rewrite.

### 5. The named reader
Direct address to a specific reader role or situation.
> For founders who've raised once and are about to do it again.
> If you've written this pitch three times already...

### 6. The reframe
Take a known concept and reframe it in 4-7 words.
> Pitch decks are case studies, not brochures.
> A landing page is a slippery slide.

### 7. The disqualifier (Bernbach)
Name the flaw on the page. Earn the trust.
> We only take three clients a quarter.
> This isn't for everyone.

### 8. The promise-with-proof
A specific outcome with a specific proof attached.
> $47M raised across 14 decks. Here's the pattern.

### 9. The before/after compression
Two beats, contrast, in one line.
> From three rounds of revisions to one decisive draft.

### 10. The curiosity hook (no clickbait)
Promise a specific payoff, with enough specificity to earn the read.
> The 12-page teardown your investors will actually read.

### 11. The name-and-stake
Name the audience and the cost of inaction.
> Founders who skip the deck rewrite raise 38% less on average.

### 12. The single-word punch (Wieden / Abbott)
One word, one image. Earns the click through confidence alone.
> Faster.
> Lemon.

## Headline craft rules

1. **3-10 words.** Target 5-7. Cut adjectives first.
2. **First word is load-bearing.** Verbs and named subjects beat articles and adjectives. "Write" beats "Effective." "Founders" beats "The."
3. **Specificity > cleverness.** A specific number, name, or stake beats a clever turn of phrase every time.
4. **Read it aloud.** If you stumble, rewrite.
5. **Strip-brand test.** Swap your brand name for a competitor's. If the headline still fits them, it's not yours.
6. **No filler openers.** Never start with "In today's...", "Imagine a...", "Let's...", "Discover...", "Welcome to...".
7. **No question-and-answer pairs as a default.** "Want X? Get Y." reads as AI. Use sparingly.
8. **No "introducing" as a lead.** Replace with the product itself or with the reader's pain.

## The 3 worked examples

### Example 1 — SaaS landing
WEAK: "Powerful Software for Modern Teams"
STRONG: "Stop losing 12 hours a week to status updates."

Why: weak version is generic ("powerful," "modern," "teams"). Strong version names a specific cost the reader feels.

### Example 2 — Course launch
WEAK: "Master the Art of Persuasive Writing"
STRONG: "Write the deck that closes the round."

Why: weak version is hype ("master," "art"). Strong version names the specific outcome.

### Example 3 — Service business
WEAK: "Transform Your Brand with Innovative Strategy"
STRONG: "We take three clients a quarter. Here's why."

Why: weak version is buzzword soup. Strong version is a Bernbach-style disqualifier that earns the read.

## When the headline is the whole ad

The Bernbach VW Beetle ads. Abbott's one-line print work. Wieden's "Just do it." Sometimes the headline IS the campaign and body copy serves only as a footnote.

Test: can you delete every word of body copy and still understand the offer? If yes, the headline is doing real work. If no, the headline is incomplete — rewrite.

## The Hegarty zag at the headline level

Before writing the headline, audit the category's default headline pattern.
- What does every competitor open with?
- What's the cliché lead?
- What's the safe move?

Now do the opposite — but only on ONE axis (length, claim, register, structure). Two contrasts = confused. One contrast = a campaign.

## Final discipline

If you've written 10 headlines and none feel right, the problem is upstream. Re-read the brief. Check the awareness state. Pick a different voice. The headline is the last move, not the first.
